How much do B2B prospect lists cost in 2026? A pricing guide

Published 2026-05-25 · ~8 min read

If you've shopped for B2B prospect lists in 2026, you've noticed prices range from $0.01 per lead to $30+ per lead. A 3,000× spread for what looks at first like the same product. It isn't the same product. The spread reflects what work is included.

This post breaks the market into four pricing tiers, what each one delivers, what they cost, and the price-quality mismatch most teams make. No vendor agenda. The goal is to help you pick the right tier for your situation.

The four pricing tiers

1. Raw scrape$0.01-$0.05Email + name + company. Often unverified.Volume senders ($1k+ emails/day) with downstream SDR or AI personalization layer

Let's walk through each.

Tier 1. Raw scrape ($0.01-$0.05/lead)

What you get: a CSV with name, email, company, and basic firmographics. Often unverified emails. No LinkedIn URLs. No signals. No personalization.

Who sells it: data brokers like Prospeo, Findymail, ListKit. Also Apollo's free tier and various LinkedIn scrapers.

When this pencils: you're already sending high-volume cold email (1,000+/day) and you have a strong downstream personalization layer. Either an SDR team writing openers per row, or a Persana/Clay-style workflow generating them programmatically. You're using the cheap data as raw fuel and adding value above.

When this DOESN'T pencil: you're going to take this list, paste it into a sequencer, write a templated "Hi {{first_name}}, I noticed {{company}} is in {{industry}}…" opener, and send. This is what most teams do and it's why reply rates have collapsed. Templated mail-merge from a $0.05/lead list converts at under 0.5% in 2026. Gmail and Outlook's reputation systems explicitly penalize the pattern (see our 2026 deliverability brief).

Tier 2. Database access ($0.05-$0.50/lead effective)

What you get: subscription to a B2B database. Apollo at $49-$149/month gives "unlimited" searches (fair-use). ZoomInfo at $15k+/year is enterprise-tier. You search, filter, export to CSV.

Who sells it: Apollo, ZoomInfo, Lusha, Cognism, RocketReach.

When this pencils: you have an SDR or sales-ops person actively searching, filtering, building lists, and feeding them into your sequencer. The value of the subscription is the platform + searchability, not the leads themselves. At Apollo's $49/mo, even pulling 200 leads/month puts you at $0.25/lead. Competitive.

When this DOESN'T pencil: you bought the subscription and you're using 5% of it. Most Apollo/ZoomInfo seats sit underused because the buying team didn't budget for the SDR time required to mine them. The subscription becomes a sunk cost that justifies more mail-merge spray. More on the Apollo math here.

Tier 3. Done-for-you researched ($1.50-$5/lead)

What you get: a CSV where every row is hand-researched (or AI-researched at human-quality). Each prospect has a verified email, LinkedIn URL, firmographics, AND a personalized opener grounded in a specific signal about their company. Cited verbatim so you can verify it.

Who sells it: productized services like Outbound-in-a-Box ($1.94/lead at the Starter tier), boutique lead-gen agencies, or freelancers on Upwork at this price point. (Older "fully outsourced SDR firms" charging $5+/lead are mostly Tier 4. See below.)

When this pencils: you're a solo founder or small team where SDR labor isn't an option, but you still need real signal-grounded openers because templated mail-merge stopped working. The math: $97 for 50 prospects = $1.94/each. Doing the same research in-house would cost ~$25/prospect in SDR labor (15-20 min × $75/hr loaded cost). You're paying ~7% of in-house cost for the same depth of work.

When this DOESN'T pencil: you genuinely have an SDR with time, OR you're sending volume so high (1,000+/day) that even pre-written openers can't compete with a Persana-style AI workflow you've already built and tuned.

Tier 4. Full SDR engagement ($15-$30+/lead effective)

What you get: an agency / outsourced SDR team that handles list-building, sending, follow-up, reply triage, and meeting booking. Some price per booked meeting ($300-$600/meeting). Others price per month ($3k-$10k/month retainer) with throughput commitments.

Who sells it: SaaS-focused agencies like Belkins, CIENCE, MartalGroup, plus dozens of smaller boutiques. Also fractional GTM partners like OutboundLeads.com.

When this pencils: you're at Series A or later, have a real sales-eligible product, and want to outsource the entire SDR function rather than hire FT. The $5k-$10k/month retainer is cheaper than a fully-loaded FT SDR ($150k+/year) for the first 6-12 months while you validate channel-market fit.

When this DOESN'T pencil: you're pre-Series A and don't have validated sales motion. Agencies in this tier need clear ICP + a working sales process to deliver value. They can't compensate for those if they're missing.

The price-quality mismatch most teams make

The most common mistake in B2B prospect-list buying isn't paying too little. It's paying mid-tier for cheap-tier output:

"We pay $49/month for Apollo, then have our SDR write templated openers from Apollo's variables, then wonder why reply rates are 0.5%."

That setup pays Tier 2 prices but produces Tier 1 quality. The list itself is fine. The opener layer is the problem. You're spending money on the platform but skipping the labor that converts.

The opposite mistake also happens. Paying Tier 3 prices for what you could do in-house:

"I have a full-time SDR sitting in Apollo all day. Why am I also paying $97 for a productized list?"

If your SDR has time, you don't need Tier 3. Tier 3 exists for teams who don't have SDR labor. Use the right tier for your situation.

The recurring-vs-one-time trap

One more pricing nuance worth flagging: subscription pricing on prospect data scales linearly with use, but one-time-per-batch pricing decouples from use.

If you pay $99/month for Apollo and only pull 50 leads in month one, you paid $1.98/lead. If you pulled 500, you paid $0.20/lead. The price scales inverse to your activity.

If you pay $97 one-time for a 50-prospect batch, you paid $1.94/lead regardless of when you use them. If you skip a month, no charge. If you triple your usage one month, you triple the cost.

For volatile, project-based, or early-stage outbound. One-time-per-batch is dramatically better unit economics. For steady-state high-volume programs, subscriptions win. Most solo founders are in the first bucket but buy subscriptions because that's the default option presented to them.

A simple decision rule

Pick your tier based on these two questions:

  1. Do you have SDR labor available?
    • Yes → Tier 1 (raw scrape) or Tier 2 (database). The SDR does the personalization work.
    • No → Tier 3 (done-for-you) or Tier 4 (full SDR engagement).
  2. What's your monthly send volume?
    • Under 500 → one-time-per-batch beats subscription.
    • 500-5,000 → either works. Pick based on your team shape.
    • 5,000+ → subscription beats one-time. The unit economics flip.

Most solo founders + 1-5 person teams land in "no SDR + under 500/month". Which is Tier 3 one-time territory. Most Series A+ teams with sales ops land in "yes SDR + 500+/month". Tier 2 subscriptions or Tier 4 engagement.
